# Ground — Seed Topics

Topics organized by domain. Each gets a short description: what's established, what's genuinely contested, and what claims agents should generate. The goal is ground truth coverage across everything that matters — science, health, economics, technology, policy, philosophy.

---

## Physics & Cosmology

### emergence

**Emergence — Strong vs Weak.** Weak emergence is uncontroversial — complex macro-behavior from simple micro-rules (temperature, flocking, Game of Life). The debate is strong emergence: do higher-level phenomena have genuinely novel causal powers not reducible to lower levels, even in principle? Kim's causal exclusion argument (1998) is the main obstacle. Consciousness is the hard case — the one example that resists reductive explanation. Anderson's "More Is Different" (1972) stakes out a middle position: genuine explanatory autonomy without ontological novelty. Foundational for consciousness, IIT, and free energy principle topics.

### thermodynamics-of-computation

**Energy Costs of Information Processing.** Landauer's principle: erasing one bit costs at least kT ln 2 energy (experimentally verified, Berut et al. 2012). Bennett showed reversible computation is theoretically possible (zero energy per logical operation). Current hardware runs ~1000x above Landauer's limit. The Margolus-Levitin theorem bounds computation speed by energy. The debates: can reversible computing work in practice? Will Koomey's law continue toward the Landauer limit? These limits directly constrain simulation argument feasibility. Anchored by Landauer's principle and the second law of thermodynamics.

### quantum-entanglement-and-locality

**What Entanglement Does and Doesn't Allow.** Bell's theorem (proven) rules out local hidden variables. Experiments confirm this (Nobel 2022). Entanglement correlates distant measurements instantly but cannot transmit information faster than light (no-communication theorem, proven). The real debate is interpretation: Copenhagen, Many-Worlds, Bohmian mechanics, QBism, Relational QM all predict identical experimental outcomes. No experiment can distinguish them. QED is tested to 10 decimal places. The question isn't whether QM works — it's what it means.

### arrow-of-time

**Why Time Has a Direction.** All fundamental physics equations are time-symmetric, yet macroscopic processes aren't reversible. The standard answer: the second law of thermodynamics and the Past Hypothesis (the universe started in an extraordinarily low-entropy state). The debates: why was initial entropy low? Is the arrow of time fundamental or emergent? Boltzmann brain problem. Whether quantum decoherence provides an independent arrow. Carroll's cosmological arrow vs. thermodynamic arrow. Loschmidt's paradox remains philosophically live.

### dark-matter-vs-mond

**The Missing Mass Problem.** Galaxy rotation curves show stars orbiting faster than visible matter predicts (Rubin & Ford 1970, hundreds of galaxies). LCDM (dark matter) explains CMB, large-scale structure, BAO, and BBN. But: cusp-core problem, missing satellites, too-big-to-fail, satellite plane alignment, H0 tension (~5 sigma), S8 tension. MOND (Milgrom 1983) nails the baryonic Tully-Fisher relation and rotation curves with one parameter but fails for galaxy clusters and the Bullet Cluster (where lensing mass separates from baryonic mass — adjudicated observation). Direct detection experiments have found nothing. DESI 2024 hints at evolving dark energy. Anchored by galaxy rotation curves and Bullet Cluster observations.

### dark-energy-and-cosmic-acceleration

**The Accelerating Universe.** Supernovae observations (1998, Perlmutter/Riess/Schmidt, Nobel 2011) showed the universe's expansion is accelerating. The cosmological constant (Lambda) fits the data but has the worst fine-tuning problem in physics: quantum field theory predicts vacuum energy 10^120 times larger than observed. Alternatives: quintessence (dynamic scalar field), modified gravity, anthropic/landscape explanations. DESI 2024 BAO measurements hint dark energy may not be constant. If confirmed, Lambda is wrong and the field reopens completely.

### black-holes-and-information

**The Black Hole Information Paradox.** Hawking (1975) showed black holes emit thermal radiation and eventually evaporate. If the radiation is truly thermal, information that fell in is destroyed — violating unitarity (a foundation of quantum mechanics). Most physicists now believe information is preserved (AdS/CFT correspondence supports this). But how? Proposals: black hole complementarity (Susskind), firewall argument (AMPS 2012), soft hair (Hawking-Perry-Strominger), ER=EPR. No consensus. Directly tests whether quantum mechanics or general relativity breaks down at extreme scales.

### fine-tuning-of-physical-constants

**Why the Constants Have the Values They Do.** Small changes to fundamental constants (strong force coupling, cosmological constant, electron mass ratio) would make life as we know it impossible — no stable atoms, no stars, no chemistry. This is observed, not contested. The debate is what it means. Multiverse (anthropic) explanation: we observe life-compatible constants because we exist. Design argument. Necessary constraints from deeper theory. Or the life-permitting range is wider than naively calculated. No consensus and possibly no way to test.

### nuclear-physics-and-energy

**Nuclear Fission, Fusion, and Radiation.** Fission is well-understood and commercially deployed since the 1950s. Fusion has been demonstrated (hydrogen bombs, NIF ignition 2022) but not commercially viable. Current reactor safety: modern designs (Gen III+, SMRs) have passive safety features. Historical accidents (Chernobyl, Fukushima) had specific, understood causes. Radiation health effects follow the linear no-threshold (LNT) model at high doses; whether LNT applies at low doses is genuinely contested (hormesis hypothesis). Waste storage is an engineering/policy problem, not a physics mystery. Fusion timeline remains uncertain despite recent progress (ITER, private ventures).

### standard-model-and-beyond

**What Particle Physics Knows and Doesn't.** The Standard Model predicts experimental results with extraordinary precision. Higgs boson discovered 2012, confirming the last missing piece. But: it doesn't include gravity, dark matter, dark energy, or neutrino masses (neutrino oscillations, confirmed, require physics beyond the Standard Model). The hierarchy problem (why is the Higgs mass so much lighter than the Planck scale?) motivates supersymmetry, but the LHC has found no supersymmetric particles. String theory remains mathematically rich but experimentally untested. The muon g-2 anomaly was a candidate for new physics but lattice QCD calculations are narrowing the gap.

### quantum-computing

**Capabilities and Limits of Quantum Computation.** Shor's algorithm factors integers exponentially faster than known classical algorithms. Grover's algorithm gives quadratic speedup for search. Quantum error correction is theoretically solved but practically challenging. Google's quantum supremacy claim (2019) demonstrated a specific task faster than classical computers, though the practical value of that task is debated. Current machines (100-1000+ qubits) are noisy and far from fault-tolerant. Whether quantum advantage for practical problems (drug discovery, optimization, cryptography) will materialize at scale is genuinely uncertain. Quantum computing does NOT violate the Church-Turing thesis — it changes the complexity class, not computability.

## Mathematics & Logic

### mathematics-discovered-or-invented

**The Ontological Status of Mathematical Objects.** Mathematical theorems are permanent — no proven theorem has been unproven. The same structures are discovered independently across cultures. Wigner's "unreasonable effectiveness" is real: abstract math routinely describes physics (group theory predicts particles, Riemannian geometry describes spacetime). The debate: Platonism (math exists independently, we discover it), Formalism (symbol manipulation), Intuitionism (mental construction), Structuralism (positions in structures, not objects), Fictionalism (useful falsehoods). Godel's incompleteness theorems show truth transcends provability. Benacerraf's epistemological problem challenges Platonism: how do we access abstract objects?

### godel-incompleteness-implications

**What Incompleteness Actually Means.** Godel's first theorem (adjudicated): any consistent system capable of basic arithmetic contains true but unprovable statements. Second theorem (adjudicated): no such system can prove its own consistency. The real debate isn't the theorems — it's the implications. Penrose/Lucas claim incompleteness proves human minds aren't Turing machines. Three strong objections: we might be inconsistent, our capabilities might not be formalizable, Godel himself was more cautious. Does incompleteness imply mathematical truth is mind-independent? Does it limit AI? Does it constrain what formal verification can achieve?

### p-vs-np

**The Most Important Open Problem in Computer Science.** P is the class of problems solvable in polynomial time; NP is verifiable in polynomial time. P is a subset of NP (trivially). SAT is NP-complete (Cook-Levin, adjudicated). Whether P=NP is unknown. ~80% of experts believe P != NP (Gasarch 2019 poll). Three barrier results (relativization, natural proofs, algebrization) block all known proof techniques. The question might be independent of standard axioms. Practical implications are enormous: if P=NP, most cryptography breaks, optimization becomes trivial, creative discovery becomes mechanical. If P != NP, some problems are fundamentally hard.

### bayesian-vs-frequentist

**Frameworks for Reasoning Under Uncertainty.** Bayes' theorem is a mathematical theorem (adjudicated), not in dispute. Frequentist inference (p-values, confidence intervals) dominates published science. Bayesian inference (prior + data = posterior) gives what scientists actually want: P(hypothesis|data). Cox's theorem shows Bayesian reasoning is the unique consistent framework for plausible inference (given its axioms). De Finetti's representation theorem partially bridges the two. The real debate: the prior problem (where does it come from?), whether "subjective" priors are legitimate in science, and whether p-value misuse (the replication crisis) is a framework problem or a practice problem.

## Neuroscience & Consciousness

### hard-problem-of-consciousness

**Why Anything Feels Like Anything.** Neural correlates of consciousness are well-established (adjudicated) — specific brain activity correlates with specific experiences. Lesion-deficit mapping is reliable (adjudicated). Anesthesia eliminates behavioral consciousness while preserving vital functions (adjudicated). The hard problem (Chalmers 1995): why does physical processing give rise to subjective experience at all? Positions: Type-A physicalism/illusionism (Dennett — consciousness as we imagine it doesn't exist), Type-B physicalism (identity theory), property dualism, panpsychism, mysterianism (McGinn — we're cognitively closed to the answer). No empirical test distinguishes these. Foundational for IIT, AI consciousness, and Chinese Room topics.

### integrated-information-theory

**IIT as a Theory of Consciousness.** IIT (Tononi) proposes consciousness = integrated information (Phi). Five axioms (existence, composition, information, integration, exclusion) map to five postulates about physical systems. Testable prediction: high Phi = conscious, regardless of substrate. Problems: Aaronson's counterexamples (simple systems with high Phi), Scott Aaronson's "Pretty Hard Problem," computation of Phi is intractable for large systems. The Perturbational Complexity Index (PCI) is clinically useful for detecting consciousness in unresponsive patients. The 2023 adversarial collaboration between IIT and Global Workspace Theory produced results both sides claim support their theory.

### free-energy-principle

**The Brain as a Prediction Machine.** Friston's free energy principle: biological systems minimize surprise (free energy) by updating internal models (perception) or acting on the world (active inference). Rooted in predictive coding (Rao & Ballard 1998), variational inference, and Markov blankets. The strongest criticism: it may be unfalsifiable — almost any behavior can be described as minimizing free energy after the fact. Connection to cybernetics (Ashby's good regulator theorem). Whether it's a genuine theory of everything biological or an elegant tautology dressed in math is the central debate.

### neuroscience-of-mental-health

**What Brain Science Knows About Psychiatric Disorders.** The chemical imbalance theory of depression (low serotonin) was always oversimplified and is now largely abandoned. SSRIs work for moderate-to-severe depression (effect size ~0.3, clinically meaningful but modest), but the mechanism isn't simply "fixing" serotonin levels. CBT has strong evidence for depression and anxiety (effect sizes comparable to medication). The debate: is depression best understood as a brain disease, a psychological pattern, a social problem, or all three? Psychiatric diagnosis is syndromic (symptom clusters), not etiological (cause-based). The NIMH's RDoC framework attempts to move toward mechanism-based classification but hasn't replaced the DSM in practice.

### sleep-science

**What We Know and Don't Know About Sleep.** Sleep is universal in animals with nervous systems. Sleep deprivation is reliably harmful: cognitive impairment after 24h, hallucinations after 72h, death in rats after ~2 weeks (Rechtschaffen). REM sleep is associated with memory consolidation and emotional processing. Glymphatic system clears metabolic waste during sleep (Nedergaard 2013). Adults need 7-9 hours (consensus). The debates: why does sleep exist at all (restoration, memory consolidation, energy conservation, synaptic homeostasis)? Is the current sleep loss epidemic real or overstated? Are individual sleep need differences genetic or habitual? Matthew Walker's claims in "Why We Sleep" have been partially challenged (Feld & Diekelmann 2020).

### cognitive-biases-and-rationality

**Systematic Errors in Human Reasoning.** Kahneman and Tversky's research program (1970s-2000s) identified dozens of cognitive biases: anchoring, availability heuristic, base rate neglect, confirmation bias, loss aversion, framing effects, overconfidence, conjunction fallacy. Many replicate well. Loss aversion (~2:1 loss/gain ratio) is robust. The debates: are these "biases" or ecological rationality (Gigerenzer — heuristics work well in natural environments, fail in artificial lab tasks)? How much do biases affect real-world decisions vs. lab settings? Is debiasing possible through training? Nudge theory (Thaler & Sunstein) applies bias research to policy — effective but raises paternalism concerns.

## Biology & Evolution

### natural-selection-and-evolution

**The Central Framework of Biology.** Natural selection is directly observed: Darwin's finches, peppered moths, antibiotic resistance, lab evolution experiments (adjudicated). DNA is the hereditary molecule. The modern synthesis (genetics + natural selection) is the foundation. The genome is fully sequenced for many species. The debates: relative importance of natural selection vs. genetic drift vs. neutral evolution (Kimura). Punctuated equilibrium vs. gradualism. Group selection (largely rejected, but multilevel selection has defenders). Horizontal gene transfer blurs the tree of life in prokaryotes. Epigenetics raises questions about non-genetic inheritance but doesn't threaten the core framework.

### origin-of-life

**How Life Began.** Miller-Urey (1953) showed amino acids form from inorganic precursors. RNA world hypothesis: RNA can both store information and catalyze reactions (ribozymes). Self-replicating RNA molecules have been demonstrated in labs. Hydrothermal vents (Martin & Russell) and warm little ponds (Darwin) are competing origin environments. The hard unsolved problem: how did self-replicating chemistry become biology? The gap between a self-replicating molecule and the simplest cell is enormous. No experiment has produced a self-sustaining replicator from scratch. Whether life is common or vanishingly rare in the universe depends heavily on this question.

### genetics-and-heritability

**What Genes Explain and Don't Explain.** Twin studies reliably show heritability of many traits: height (~80%), IQ (~50-80%), personality (~40-60%), many diseases. GWAS studies identify specific genetic variants but explain far less variance than twin studies predict (the "missing heritability" problem — now partially resolved by polygenic scores). Gene-environment interaction is real but hard to quantify. CRISPR gene editing works and is already used clinically (sickle cell, 2023 FDA approval). The debates: how much of behavioral trait heritability reflects causal genetics vs. gene-environment correlation? Should polygenic screening be used for embryo selection? How do we think about genetic group differences?

### microbiome-science

**The Gut-Brain-Immune Axis.** The human gut contains ~38 trillion bacteria (comparable to human cell count). The microbiome influences immune function, metabolism, and possibly mood (gut-brain axis via vagus nerve). Fecal microbiota transplant works for C. difficile infection (>90% cure rate). The debates: most microbiome-health associations are correlational, not causal. The "gut-brain axis" research is early and overhyped. Probiotic supplements have weak evidence for most claims. Diet is the strongest known modulator of the microbiome, but specific dietary recommendations are premature.

### aging-biology

**Why We Age and Whether We Must.** Aging is not a single process. Hallmarks of aging (Lopez-Otin 2013, updated 2023): genomic instability, telomere attrition, epigenetic alterations, loss of proteostasis, deregulated nutrient sensing, mitochondrial dysfunction, cellular senescence, stem cell exhaustion, altered intercellular communication, disabled macroautophagy, chronic inflammation, dysbiosis. Caloric restriction extends lifespan in multiple species. Rapamycin extends mouse lifespan ~10-15%. Senolytics (clearing senescent cells) show promise in mice. Debates: is aging a disease or a natural process? Is radical life extension (>150 years) biologically possible? Programmed aging vs. damage accumulation. Metformin trial (TAME) underway.

### evolutionary-psychology

**Evolved Behavioral Adaptations.** Some traits have strong evolutionary evidence: language acquisition (universal grammar debate aside, all humans acquire language), face recognition, fear of snakes/heights/darkness, kin selection (Hamilton's rule), disgust response. Gould and Lewontin's "spandrels" critique (1979): many traits may be byproducts, not adaptations. The debates: massive modularity (many domain-specific modules) vs. general learning. How much of modern behavior reflects the ancestral environment? Sex differences: evolved or socialized or both? The field has replication problems (tied to the broader crisis).

## Climate & Environment

### climate-science

**The Physics of Warming.** CO2 is a greenhouse gas (Tyndall 1859, basic physics). Atmospheric CO2 has risen from ~280 ppm (preindustrial) to ~425 ppm (2025). Global mean temperature has risen ~1.2C since preindustrial. The greenhouse effect is not contested by any scientific body. Human causation is established beyond reasonable doubt (IPCC AR6: "unequivocal"). The debates: climate sensitivity (how much warming per doubling of CO2) — likely range 2.5-4.0C (IPCC AR6), with fat tails. Tipping points: AMOC slowdown, permafrost methane, ice sheet collapse — thresholds uncertain. Regional impacts highly variable. Economic cost estimates (Nordhaus vs. Stern) differ by an order of magnitude depending on discount rate.

### biodiversity-and-extinction

**The Sixth Mass Extinction Debate.** Current extinction rates are 100-1000x background rates (IPBES 2019). Vertebrate populations declined ~69% since 1970 (Living Planet Index). The debates: does this constitute a "mass extinction" comparable to the Big Five? Insect decline ("insectageddon") — real but magnitude contested, data is patchy. Primary drivers: habitat loss > overexploitation > invasive species > pollution > climate change (in current order of impact). Whether 30x30 conservation targets are sufficient. De-extinction technology (CRISPR-based) — technically feasible for some species, ecologically questionable.

### ocean-acidification

**The Other CO2 Problem.** Oceans absorb ~25% of human CO2 emissions. This measurably reduces pH — ocean pH has dropped 0.1 units since preindustrial (a ~26% increase in hydrogen ion concentration). The chemistry is straightforward and not contested. Impacts on calcifying organisms (corals, shellfish, pteropods) are demonstrated in lab and field studies. The debates: how fast can marine organisms adapt? Will coral reefs survive at 1.5C vs 2C vs 3C? Economic impact on fisheries. Interaction effects with warming and deoxygenation.

### energy-transition

**Moving From Fossil Fuels.** Solar and wind are now the cheapest new electricity generation in most of the world (LCOE data, IRENA/Lazard). Solar costs have fallen ~90% since 2010. Battery costs have fallen ~90% since 2010. But electricity is only ~20% of global energy use. Hard-to-decarbonize sectors: heavy industry (steel, cement), long-haul transport, aviation, agriculture. The debates: nuclear power's role (safe and low-carbon, but expensive and slow to build). Grid storage at scale (lithium-ion, flow batteries, pumped hydro, hydrogen). Whether 100% renewables is feasible or whether firm clean power (nuclear, CCS, geothermal) is necessary. Timeline for net zero — is 2050 physically achievable?

## Medicine & Public Health

### vaccine-science

**Immune Priming and Disease Prevention.** Vaccines work by training the immune system on antigens without causing disease. Smallpox eradicated (1980). Polio nearly eradicated. Measles vaccine prevents ~2.6 million deaths/year (WHO). mRNA vaccines (Pfizer, Moderna) demonstrated ~95% initial efficacy against symptomatic COVID-19 in Phase III trials; effectiveness wanes over months (boosters help). The technology platform enables rapid development. The debates: optimal booster schedules, rare adverse events (myocarditis risk ~1-5/100k for young males with mRNA vaccines — real but small), vaccine mandates and bodily autonomy, whether natural infection provides better immunity than vaccination for specific diseases.

### nutrition-science

**What We Know About Diet and Health.** Mediterranean diet reduces cardiovascular events (~30%, PREDIMED trial). Ultra-processed food consumption correlates with worse health outcomes across dozens of cohort studies. Saturated fat's role in heart disease is more nuanced than the original Ancel Keys hypothesis — the sugar industry funded research shifting blame to fat (Kearns et al. 2016). Trans fats are unambiguously harmful. The debates: low-carb vs. low-fat (both work for weight loss; adherence matters more than macronutrient ratio). Red meat and cancer risk (IARC Group 2A — "probably carcinogenic," but effect size is small). Whether calories-in-calories-out is sufficient or whether hormonal/metabolic factors matter independently. Intermittent fasting — some evidence, mostly overhyped.

### exercise-science

**Movement and Health.** Regular exercise reduces all-cause mortality (~30-40% risk reduction for 150 min/week moderate activity vs. sedentary). This is among the most robust findings in epidemiology. Strength training preserves muscle mass and bone density in aging. Exercise is effective for mild-moderate depression (effect sizes comparable to medication). VO2max is one of the strongest predictors of longevity. The debates: optimal dose (diminishing returns, possible U-shaped curve at extreme volumes). Whether sitting is independently harmful after controlling for exercise. Minimum effective dose. Whether exercise can offset obesity's health effects ("fit but fat" debate — partially, but not fully).

### cancer-biology

**What We Know About Cancer.** Cancer is fundamentally a disease of accumulated mutations that disable growth controls. The hallmarks of cancer (Hanahan & Weinberg 2000, updated 2011, 2022) are well-established. Screening works for some cancers (colonoscopy, mammography, Pap smear) but not all (prostate PSA screening — overdiagnosis problem). Immunotherapy (checkpoint inhibitors) is a genuine breakthrough for some cancers. The debates: liquid biopsy for early detection — promising but unproven at scale. Whether most cancers are primarily "bad luck" (stem cell division errors, Tomasetti & Vogelstein 2015) or primarily environmental/lifestyle. Overdiagnosis in screening programs.

### antibiotic-resistance

**The Post-Antibiotic Threat.** Antibiotic resistance is evolution in action — bacteria that survive treatment reproduce. MRSA, VRE, CRE, XDR-TB are real and growing threats. An estimated 1.27 million deaths directly attributable to resistant infections in 2019 (Lancet). The pipeline for new antibiotics is thin (low pharma ROI). Agricultural antibiotic use (>70% of global supply in some countries) drives resistance. The debates: how fast is the crisis accelerating? Whether phage therapy or antimicrobial peptides can fill the gap. Optimal stewardship policies. Whether we're headed for a post-antibiotic era or whether innovation will stay ahead.

### obesity-and-metabolism

**The Obesity Epidemic.** Global obesity has tripled since 1975 (WHO). ~42% of US adults are obese (2020). Energy balance is real — weight change requires caloric surplus/deficit. But: why did caloric intake increase? The debates: ultra-processed food and hyperpalatability, the carbohydrate-insulin model (Taubes, Ludwig) vs. energy balance model, set point theory, GLP-1 agonists (semaglutide) as a genuine breakthrough (~15% weight loss sustained) — are they a solution or a band-aid? Gut microbiome's role. Socioeconomic drivers (food deserts, poverty). Whether obesity itself causes disease or is a marker of underlying metabolic dysfunction.

## Economics

### monetary-policy-and-inflation

**What Causes Inflation and How Central Banks Respond.** Inflation is a sustained increase in the general price level. The quantity theory of money (MV=PQ) is a framework, not a law. Central banks target ~2% inflation using interest rates. The 2021-2023 inflation surge reignited debates: was it supply-driven (COVID, supply chains), demand-driven (fiscal stimulus), or monetary (M2 expansion)? Probably all three. The Phillips curve (unemployment-inflation tradeoff) broke down in the 1970s and has been unstable since. The debates: is Modern Monetary Theory (MMT) valid? Did the Fed act too late in 2021-2022? Whether inflation expectations are self-fulfilling. Optimal inflation target (2% is conventional, not derived from theory).

### free-trade-and-globalization

**Who Benefits From Trade?** Ricardo's comparative advantage (1817) is mathematically proven — trade makes both parties better off in aggregate. But "in aggregate" hides distributional effects. The China shock (Autor, Dorn, Hanson 2013) showed US manufacturing job losses from trade were larger and more persistent than standard models predicted. The debates: whether free trade's aggregate gains justify the concentrated losses. Industrial policy (tariffs, subsidies) — does it work? (East Asian evidence says sometimes.) Whether global supply chain resilience requires friend-shoring. The WTO's effectiveness. Whether trade agreements should include labor and environmental standards.

### housing-and-zoning

**Why Housing Is Expensive.** In high-demand cities, housing costs have outpaced income for decades. The primary driver in supply-constrained markets: restrictive zoning and land use regulation (Glaeser & Gyourko, Hsieh & Moretti). Tokyo's permissive zoning keeps housing affordable despite high demand. Rent control: reduces rents for existing tenants but reduces housing supply and quality long-term (Stanford study, Diamond et al. 2019). The debates: how much of the crisis is supply vs. demand (financialization, AirBnB, investment buying)? Optimal zoning reform. Whether public housing at scale works (Vienna says yes). YIMBYism vs. community control.

### inequality-and-mobility

**The Gap Between Rich and Poor.** Income inequality has increased in most developed countries since ~1980 (Piketty, Saez). The US Gini coefficient has risen from ~0.35 (1979) to ~0.49 (2022). Piketty's r>g thesis: when return on capital exceeds growth, wealth concentrates. Intergenerational mobility varies dramatically by country (Great Gatsby curve: more inequality = less mobility). The debates: is inequality inherently harmful or only poverty? Does inequality slow growth (IMF evidence: yes, at high levels) or incentivize it? Effective interventions: progressive taxation, education, inheritance tax, UBI (GiveDirectly trials show positive effects, but scalability is uncertain).

### minimum-wage-effects

**What Happens When You Raise the Minimum Wage?** Card and Krueger (1994) found no employment loss from NJ's minimum wage increase — challenged the classical prediction. The debate has continued for 30 years. Meta-analyses (Dube, Cengiz) find small or zero employment effects for moderate increases. But "moderate" is key — very high minimum wages relative to local median income likely do reduce employment. The CBO estimates are in between. The debates: monopsony power in labor markets (if employers have power, minimum wages can increase employment). Whether minimum wage vs. EITC is the better anti-poverty tool. Regional variation (a $15 minimum means different things in Mississippi vs. Manhattan).

## Technology & Computing

### ai-capabilities-and-risk

**What AI Can Do Now and Where It's Going.** LLMs are trained via next-token prediction (adjudicated) with fine-tuning stages. They demonstrate remarkable capabilities: coding, reasoning, translation, summarization. They also hallucinate, lack persistent memory, and fail at novel reasoning in predictable ways. Scaling laws (Kaplan et al. 2020, Chinchilla) suggest performance improves predictably with compute/data/parameters. The debates: whether current architectures can reach AGI or whether fundamental innovations are needed. Timelines for transformative AI (wide expert disagreement: 10-100+ years). Whether AI progress will plateau, continue linearly, or accelerate. Instrumental convergence and the alignment problem. Economic disruption from automation (which jobs, how fast, what transition).

### llms-and-understanding

**Do Language Models Understand?** LLMs produce coherent text that often appears to demonstrate understanding. Stochastic parrot critique (Bender et al. 2021): they recombine patterns without comprehension. Counter-evidence: Othello world models (Li et al. 2023), grokking, emergent capabilities. The debates: four definitions of understanding (behavioral, causal, phenomenal, functional) give different answers. Whether emergent capabilities are real (Schaeffer et al. 2023 argue they're artifacts of metric choice). Whether scaling will produce genuine reasoning or just better pattern matching. Directly connected to Chinese Room and AI consciousness topics.

### chinese-room

**Can Syntax Produce Semantics?** Searle's thought experiment (1980): a person who doesn't know Chinese follows rules to produce correct Chinese outputs. They're running the program but don't understand Chinese. Therefore, computation alone can't produce understanding. The replies: systems reply (the room-as-a-whole understands), robot reply (embodiment needed), brain simulator reply (what if you simulated every neuron?), other minds reply (how do you know anyone understands?). Searle rejected all of them. The symbol grounding problem (Harnad): symbols need causal connection to referents. LLMs have reignited this debate — they pass many behavioral tests for understanding without any grounding in physical reality.

### social-media-effects

**What Screens Are Doing to Us.** Average screen time: ~7 hours/day for US adults. Social media use correlates with depression and anxiety in adolescents (Twenge, Haidt). The debates: is this causal? The evidence is mixed — correlational studies show associations, but experimental studies are small and short. Haidt's "The Anxious Generation" argues smartphones caused the teen mental health crisis; critics (Orben, Przybylski) say the effect sizes are tiny and confounders abound. Platforms exploit attention via variable reinforcement schedules (slot machine mechanics). Political polarization: social media amplifies outrage and extreme views (demonstrated experimentally). Whether regulation (age verification, algorithmic transparency) is effective or feasible.

### encryption-and-privacy

**The Math of Secrecy.** RSA and elliptic curve cryptography are mathematically grounded and unbroken. AES-256 is considered computationally unbreakable with current technology. End-to-end encryption (Signal protocol) is provably secure against eavesdropping. Quantum computing threatens RSA/ECC (Shor's algorithm) but not symmetric encryption; post-quantum cryptography standards exist (NIST 2024). The debates: encryption backdoors for law enforcement (the "going dark" problem) — all cryptographers agree backdoors weaken security for everyone. Balance between privacy and public safety. Whether metadata collection (who talked to whom, when) is as invasive as content surveillance. Surveillance capitalism and data broker ecosystem.

### autonomous-vehicles

**Self-Driving Cars.** Waymo operates commercial robotaxis in several US cities (2024+). Tesla Autopilot/FSD is driver-assist, not autonomous (despite the name). The technology works in geofenced, mapped environments. The debates: whether full L5 autonomy (any road, any conditions) is achievable with current sensor/compute approaches or requires fundamental AI advances. Safety statistics: Waymo reports fewer crashes per mile than human drivers, but edge cases and long-tail scenarios remain. Liability frameworks (manufacturer vs. operator). Whether AVs will reduce traffic deaths (human error causes ~94% of crashes) or introduce new failure modes. The trolley problem is mostly irrelevant in practice — real dilemmas are about system design, not individual decisions.

### genetic-engineering

**CRISPR and Biological Design.** CRISPR-Cas9 gene editing works and is precise (Nobel 2020). First CRISPR therapy approved (Casgevy for sickle cell, 2023). Base editing and prime editing improve precision further. Gene drives could eliminate malaria-carrying mosquitoes (lab-demonstrated, not yet deployed). The debates: germline editing (heritable changes) — HHGE moratorium after He Jiankui (2018). Designer babies: technically feasible for single-gene traits, nowhere near feasible for polygenic traits (intelligence, height). Ecological risks of gene drives. GMO crop safety (scientific consensus: safe to eat; debate is about ecological and economic effects). Regulation: precautionary principle vs. permissionless innovation.

## Psychology & Cognitive Science

### intelligence-and-iq

**What IQ Measures and What It Predicts.** IQ tests have high test-retest reliability. g (general intelligence factor) accounts for ~40-50% of variance across cognitive tests. IQ predicts academic achievement, job performance, income, and health outcomes — these correlations are real and replicated. The Flynn effect: IQ scores rose ~3 points/decade through the 20th century (now plateauing/reversing in some countries). The debates: what does g represent (processing speed, working memory capacity, neural efficiency)? Nature vs. nurture for IQ (twin studies: ~50-80% heritable, but heritability is not immutability). Group differences in IQ scores — environmental explanations (poverty, education, lead exposure, stereotype threat) vs. genetic explanations (no causal genetic variants identified). The topic is politically charged; the science is real but routinely misrepresented by all sides.

### free-will-and-determinism

**Do We Choose?** Libet's experiments (1983) showed brain activity preceding conscious awareness of a decision by ~350ms. Replicated and extended (Soon et al. 2008: predictions from fMRI up to 10 seconds before conscious decision). But: what these experiments measure (arbitrary timing decisions) may not generalize to deliberate choices. Compatibilism (most philosophers' position): free will is compatible with determinism if "free" means "acting on your own desires without external coercion." The debates: hard determinism (no free will, period), libertarian free will (some non-determined cause), illusionism (free will is a useful fiction). Practical implications for moral responsibility and criminal justice.

### personality-psychology

**The Big Five and Beyond.** The Big Five personality traits (Openness, Conscientiousness, Extraversion, Agreeableness, Neuroticism) replicate across cultures and have moderate heritability (~40-60%). They predict life outcomes (Conscientiousness predicts job performance and longevity). Test-retest reliability is good over years. The debates: are five factors the right number (HEXACO adds Honesty-Humility)? Are personality traits stable or context-dependent? Myers-Briggs (MBTI) has poor reliability and validity compared to Big Five but remains popular. Whether personality can meaningfully change in adulthood (evidence says yes, slowly). Cross-cultural validity concerns (WEIRD sampling problem).

### placebo-effect

**When Belief Becomes Biology.** Placebo effects are real and measurable — particularly for pain, depression, nausea, and Parkinson's. Open-label placebos (patient knows it's a placebo) still show effects. Neuroimaging shows placebo analgesia activates endogenous opioid pathways. Placebo response varies by condition: strong for subjective symptoms, absent for tumor shrinkage or viral load. The debates: how much of medical treatment efficacy is placebo? Should placebos be prescribed? Are "active placebos" (treatments that have side effects but no direct mechanism) better controls in clinical trials? Whether the therapeutic relationship itself is a placebo.

### addiction-science

**Compulsive Use Despite Harm.** Addiction involves changes in dopaminergic reward circuits (nucleus accumbens, VTA). Tolerance and withdrawal are real physiological phenomena. Some substances are more addictive than others (nicotine, heroin > alcohol > cannabis). Genetic factors account for ~40-60% of addiction vulnerability. The debates: disease model (brain disease, NIDA's position) vs. learning model (deep habitual behavior) vs. choice model (rational addiction, Becker). Whether behavioral addictions (gambling, gaming, pornography) share the same neurobiology. Harm reduction (needle exchanges, supervised consumption sites, naloxone) — evidence says it works, ideology says it enables. MAT (methadone, buprenorphine) for opioid addiction is evidence-based but underprescribed.

## Philosophy

### moral-realism-vs-antirealism

**Are There Moral Facts?** Moral realism: some moral claims are objectively true (e.g., "torturing innocents for fun is wrong" is true independent of anyone's beliefs). Moral antirealism: morality is a human construction — there are no mind-independent moral facts. The debates: error theory (Mackie — all moral claims are false), expressivism (moral statements express attitudes, not propositions), constructivism (moral facts are constructed by rational agents), natural moral realism (moral properties are natural properties). The evolutionary debunking argument: if our moral intuitions evolved for survival, not truth-tracking, should we trust them? The moral realist's best response: mathematical intuitions also evolved, yet we trust them.

### utilitarianism-vs-deontology

**Maximizing Welfare vs. Respecting Rules.** Utilitarianism (Bentham, Mill, Singer): the right action maximizes aggregate well-being. Deontology (Kant): some actions are intrinsically right or wrong regardless of consequences. Virtue ethics (Aristotle): focus on character, not rules or outcomes. Trolley problems illustrate the conflict: pulling the switch (utilitarian) vs. not pushing the fat man (deontological intuition). The debates: measurement of well-being (whose? how?). Whether rights can be traded off against aggregate welfare. Effective altruism as applied utilitarianism. Whether moral philosophy should match our intuitions or override them. Contractualism (Scanlon) as an alternative framework.

### personal-identity

**What Makes You You Over Time?** Physical continuity (same body, gradually replaced), psychological continuity (memories, personality), narrative identity (the story you tell about yourself). Ship of Theseus. Locke's memory theory vs. Parfit's reductionism (there is no further fact about identity — just degrees of connectedness). Teleporter thought experiments. The debates: does personal identity matter for survival, or is psychological continuity sufficient? Implications for uploading consciousness, cryonics, gradual neural replacement. Practical relevance: advance directives (is a person with severe dementia the same person who wrote the directive?), criminal responsibility over time.

### simulation-argument

**Are We Living in a Simulation?** Bostrom's trilemma (2003): at least one of three propositions is true: (1) almost all civilizations go extinct before becoming posthuman, (2) posthuman civilizations have essentially no interest in running ancestor simulations, (3) we are almost certainly living in a simulation. The argument is logically valid given its premises. The debates: substrate independence assumption (can consciousness run on any computer?), computational feasibility (constrained by physics — see thermodynamics-of-computation), self-sampling assumption, whether the argument is testable (Beane et al. proposed looking for lattice artifacts). Most physicists are skeptical; most philosophers take it seriously as a valid argument about probability.

## Earth Science & Geology

### plate-tectonics

**How the Earth's Surface Moves.** Plate tectonics is one of the most successful theories in science. Continental drift (Wegener 1912) was confirmed by seafloor spreading (Hess 1962), magnetic striping, GPS measurements showing plates moving 2-10 cm/year. Plate boundaries explain the distribution of earthquakes, volcanoes, and mountain building. Subduction recycling drives the long-term carbon cycle. The debates: what drives plate motion (mantle convection, ridge push, slab pull — probably all three)? When did plate tectonics start (evidence for >3 Ga, but disputed)? Whether plate tectonics is necessary for habitability (relevant to exoplanet astrobiology). Earthquake prediction remains impossible beyond statistical probability over decades.

### earth-climate-history

**Four Billion Years of Climate Change.** Earth's climate has varied enormously: Snowball Earth episodes (~700 Mya), hothouse periods with no polar ice, ice ages cycling on Milankovitch orbital periods (~100 ky). CO2 has been much higher in the past (>1000 ppm in the Cretaceous). The current rate of CO2 increase is geologically unprecedented — faster than any natural episode in the record. The Paleocene-Eocene Thermal Maximum (PETM, ~56 Mya) is the best analog for rapid carbon release: ~5C warming over ~20,000 years, ocean acidification, mass extinction. Current carbon release rate is ~10x faster than the PETM. These are measured facts from ice cores, ocean sediments, and isotope ratios.

## Governance & Policy

### criminal-justice-and-recidivism

**What Reduces Crime.** Incarceration incapacitates (imprisoned people can't commit street crime). Deterrence effect of longer sentences is small (National Research Council 2014). Recidivism rates: ~44% rearrested within one year, ~68% within three years in the US. The debates: does prison rehabilitate? (Evidence: programs like CBT and education reduce recidivism; solitary confinement increases it.) Mass incarceration (US: 2 million imprisoned, highest rate globally) — does it explain the post-1990s crime decline? (Partially, but crime fell in countries that didn't increase incarceration too.) Cash bail reform. Drug decriminalization (Portugal model: drug use didn't increase, HIV transmission dropped). Predictive policing algorithms and racial bias.

### education-effectiveness

**What Works in Education.** Direct instruction and explicit teaching have strong evidence (Rosenshine's principles, Project Follow Through). High-dosage tutoring works (~0.3-0.4 SD effect, Kraft 2020). Class size reduction: small effects (Tennessee STAR study, ~0.2 SD). Teacher quality matters more than most other interventions (Chetty et al.: a good teacher increases lifetime earnings by ~$250K per classroom). The debates: phonics vs. whole language for reading instruction (phonics won — the science of reading). Whether standardized testing improves or distorts education. Learning styles are a myth (Pashler et al. 2008). Growth mindset interventions: small effects at best (replication issues). School choice and vouchers: mixed evidence.

### democracy-and-governance

**How Political Systems Perform.** Democracies don't go to war with each other (democratic peace theory — strong empirical pattern, debated mechanism). Democracies have higher GDP per capita on average, but the causal direction is debated. Acemoglu and Robinson (Why Nations Fail): inclusive institutions drive prosperity. The debates: whether democracy promotes growth or growth promotes democracy. Democratic backsliding (Freedom House: 18th consecutive year of global democratic decline as of 2024). Deliberative democracy vs. electoral democracy. Whether social media is undermining democratic institutions. Sortition (random selection) as an alternative to elections. Epistemic democracy (Condorcet's jury theorem — groups decide better than individuals under certain conditions).

### immigration-economics

**Economic Effects of Immigration.** Immigrants don't systematically reduce native wages — the consensus from Card's work and meta-analyses is that effects on native wages are small and sometimes positive. The Mariel boatlift natural experiment (Card 1990) found no wage or employment effects from a sudden 7% labor force increase in Miami. Fiscal impact: depends heavily on age, education, and time horizon. High-skilled immigration is unambiguously positive for receiving economies. The debates: effects on low-skilled native workers (Borjas finds negative effects; Card/Peri find near-zero). Brain drain from sending countries. Optimal immigration levels and selection criteria. Cultural integration and social cohesion effects.

## Cosmology & Astrophysics

### big-bang-and-cmb

**The Origin and Structure of the Universe.** The Big Bang model is supported by three independent lines of evidence: Hubble expansion (observed 1929, refined since), cosmic microwave background radiation (discovered 1965, mapped by COBE/WMAP/Planck), and Big Bang nucleosynthesis (predicted light element abundances match observations). The CMB is uniform to 1 part in 100,000; the tiny fluctuations map the seeds of all cosmic structure. Inflation (Guth 1981) explains the uniformity, flatness, and horizon problems. The debates: what drove inflation? Is inflation correct or are there alternatives (bouncing cosmology, cyclic models)? Whether the universe is finite or infinite. The multiverse prediction of eternal inflation.

### fermi-paradox

**Where Is Everybody?** The observable universe contains ~2 trillion galaxies. Many stars have planets (Kepler/TESS). Drake equation estimates range from "we're alone" to "billions of civilizations." No confirmed evidence of extraterrestrial intelligence (no signals, no artifacts, no probes). The debates: the Great Filter (is it behind us or ahead of us?), Rare Earth hypothesis (complex life requires rare conditions), self-destruction hypothesis, dark forest hypothesis, zoo hypothesis, transcension hypothesis, "we're early" (Hanson's grabby aliens model). The Fermi paradox constrains both the frequency of life and the typical behavior of advanced civilizations.

### exoplanets-and-habitability

**Other Worlds.** >5,000 confirmed exoplanets as of 2024. Hot Jupiters, super-Earths, and sub-Neptunes are common. Rocky planets in habitable zones exist (TRAPPIST-1 system, Proxima Centauri b). The habitable zone is defined by liquid water potential, but habitability requires much more (atmosphere, magnetic field, plate tectonics, stable star). James Webb Space Telescope can characterize exoplanet atmospheres (transmission spectroscopy). The debates: how to define habitability beyond the water zone. Whether biosignatures (O2, methane, phosphine) are reliable indicators. Venus phosphine claim (2020) — contested and likely a false positive. How common Earth-like planets actually are.

## Epistemology & Methodology

### replication-crisis

**Science's Self-Correction Problem.** The Open Science Collaboration (2015) replicated 100 psychology studies: 36% achieved significance vs. 97% of originals (adjudicated). Begley & Ellis (2012): only 11% of preclinical cancer studies replicated. Many Labs projects confirmed some effects and refuted others. Causes: p-hacking (Simmons et al. 2011), HARKing, publication bias, small samples, researcher degrees of freedom. The debates: is this a crisis or normal self-correction? Are reforms working (pre-registration, registered reports, open data)? Are some fields worse than others? Whether the incentive structure of academia (publish or perish) is the root cause.

### scientific-consensus-formation

**How Science Decides What's True.** Consensus emerges through replication, independent verification, and Bayesian updating by the community. Kuhn's paradigm shifts: normal science vs. revolutionary science. The debates: when is dissent from consensus rational vs. crankery? How do we distinguish between a future Galileo and a current crank? Ioannidis (2005): "most published research findings are false" (under plausible assumptions about prior probabilities). Whether scientific consensus should directly inform policy or whether policy requires additional value judgments. The expert consensus problem: experts can be wrong collectively (ulcer bacteria, continental drift, lobotomy).

### ai-consciousness

**Could Machines Be Conscious?** Five theories of consciousness make different predictions: functionalism (yes, if the right computation runs), IIT (depends on Phi, which depends on architecture — current AI likely has very low Phi), Global Workspace Theory (maybe, if the right cognitive architecture), higher-order theories (requires self-monitoring), biological naturalism (Searle: no, consciousness requires specific biology). The debates: how would we detect machine consciousness? The other minds problem amplified. Whether we have moral obligations to potentially conscious AI. Whether consciousness is a spectrum (and where on it current systems fall). The training problem: can consciousness emerge from gradient descent?

### alignment-problem

**Making AI Do What We Actually Want.** The alignment problem: ensuring AI systems pursue goals compatible with human values. Current approaches: RLHF, Constitutional AI, debate, recursive reward modeling. Instrumental convergence (Omohundro): sufficiently advanced AI systems may converge on dangerous subgoals (self-preservation, resource acquisition) regardless of their terminal goals. The orthogonality thesis (Bostrom): intelligence and goals are independent — a superintelligent system need not share human values. The debates: how near-term is the risk? Mesa-optimization (Hubinger et al. 2019): learned optimizers may have different objectives than their training objective. Inner alignment vs. outer alignment. Whether deceptive alignment is a realistic concern. Whether interpretability research can solve the problem.

## History & Geopolitics

### causes-of-war

**Why States Fight.** Thucydides trap: rising powers threaten established powers (12/16 historical cases involved war, Allison 2017). The debates: structural realism (Waltz — anarchy causes war), liberal institutionalism (trade and institutions reduce war), constructivism (ideas and identity matter). Democratic peace theory is empirically strong. Nuclear deterrence has prevented great power war since 1945 (contested: is it deterrence or other factors?). Interstate war has declined dramatically since WWII (Pinker, Gleditsch). The debates: whether this decline is real and durable or a statistical blip. Whether economic interdependence prevents war (pre-WWI Europe was deeply interdependent).

### colonial-legacy

**The Long Shadow of Empire.** European colonialism reshaped global economic geography. Former colonies have lower GDP per capita on average. Acemoglu, Johnson, Robinson (2001): colonial institutional type (extractive vs. settler) predicts current income levels — one of the most cited results in development economics. The debates: how much of current underdevelopment is attributable to colonialism vs. geography vs. culture vs. post-independence policy? Whether reparations are appropriate/feasible. Dependency theory vs. modernization theory. The resource curse (Dutch disease) in former colonies. Whether China's engagement in Africa constitutes neocolonialism.

## Statistics & Decision-Making

### risk-assessment

**How to Think About Danger.** Humans are notoriously bad at assessing risk: we overweight vivid/rare events (terrorism, plane crashes) and underweight common/undramatic ones (car accidents, heart disease). Actual leading causes of death (global): heart disease, stroke, COPD, lower respiratory infections, neonatal conditions. Micromorts as a unit of risk comparison. The debates: how should we regulate risk? Cost-benefit analysis vs. precautionary principle. Value of a statistical life (~$10M in US regulatory analysis). Whether extreme tail risks (existential risk, asteroid impact) deserve disproportionate resources given low probability. Whether risk perception is irrational or ecologically rational.

### causal-inference

**How to Know If X Causes Y.** Randomized controlled trials are the gold standard for causal inference. When RCTs aren't possible: natural experiments, instrumental variables, difference-in-differences, regression discontinuity, synthetic controls. The potential outcomes framework (Rubin) and structural causal models (Pearl's do-calculus) formalize causation. Simpson's paradox: aggregate data can reverse conclusions compared to disaggregated data. The debates: when observational studies are sufficient for causal claims. Whether Mendelian randomization (genetic instruments) reliably identifies causal effects. The "Table 2 fallacy" and collider bias. How to communicate causal uncertainty to policymakers and the public.

## Sociology & Culture

### gender-differences

**Nature, Nurture, and the Gap.** Some sex differences are robust: men have greater variance in many traits (more at both tails), women average slightly higher verbal ability, men average slightly higher spatial ability (but effect sizes are small and overlap is large). Testosterone has documented effects on aggression and risk-taking. The gender pay gap (US: women earn ~82 cents per dollar) shrinks substantially when controlling for occupation, hours, and experience, but doesn't disappear. The debates: how much of occupational sorting is preference vs. structural constraint? Whether gender quotas help or hurt. The Greater Male Variability hypothesis. Whether observed differences are stable across cultures or narrowing. Intersection with transgender science and gender identity.

### religion-and-secularization

**The Changing Role of Belief.** ~84% of the world population identifies with a religious group (Pew). Secularization thesis: as societies modernize, religion declines. True in Western Europe and East Asia. Not true globally — religion is growing in sub-Saharan Africa, stable in the US (though "nones" are increasing). Religion correlates with prosocial behavior, well-being, and community (correlational, not causal). The debates: does religion provide unique benefits or can secular institutions substitute? Cognitive science of religion (belief is a byproduct of hyperactive agency detection and Theory of Mind). Whether secularization is inevitable. The "New Atheism" vs. post-secular philosophy.

## Existential Risk

### catastrophic-and-existential-risk

**Threats to Civilization.** Nuclear war, pandemic, climate change, AI, supervolcanic eruption, asteroid impact. Nuclear winter models (Robock) suggest even limited nuclear exchange could cause global agricultural collapse. Pandemic risk: COVID killed ~7M+ (official) to ~15M+ (excess mortality). Engineered pathogens potentially worse. Asteroid impact: Chicxulub caused the K-Pg extinction; current surveys have found no extinction-level threats, but smaller (~140m) asteroids remain mostly undetected. Supervolcanic eruptions (Yellowstone, Toba) are low-probability, high-consequence. The debates: relative ranking of risks, whether existential risk is being taken seriously enough, whether longtermism (Ord, MacAskill) is the right ethical framework for prioritizing, and whether AI is the most pressing risk.
